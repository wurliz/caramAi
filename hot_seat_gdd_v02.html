Hot Seat — Game Design Document

v0.2 — Updated design direction

3D Simulator · PC 2 devs · part-time Passion project AI-powered NPC
Player fantasy
A hostage negotiator who unravels the fractured psychology of a desperate individual — one careful word at a time — while piecing together the truth from the evidence strewn around them.
Core mechanics
1
InvestigatePlayer freely looks around a crime-scene-style room using mouse/WASD. Objects are highlighted on hover. Clicking an object adds a clue to the player's dossier.
2
Speak updatedPlayer speaks with their actual voice via microphone. Speech is transcribed and fed to the AI character. A phone/radio UI renders the interaction, with the hostage taker visible via in-world CCTV feed or video call. The player is talking to a person they can see, not a disembodied voice.
3
Read the room updatedTension is not shown as a meter — the hostage taker's behaviour, voice, and body language are the only feedback. Lighting, ambient sound, and the character's posture/composure shift as the situation evolves. Note: a numeric tension meter will be included in v1 and removed once environmental feedback feels sufficient.
4
Unlock context updatedClue discovery and conversation progress both contribute equally to opening new dialogue directions. Clues don't gate specific topics — instead they enrich what you know and what the AI can plausibly react to. If the player talks their way into a subject without finding the physical evidence, that's valid. Finding evidence deepens the angle but doesn't block entry to it.
5
ResolveSituation ends in one of several outcomes determined by accumulated tension, clues unlocked, and key conversational moments — not a single binary choice.
Win / lose conditions updated
De-escalation (win)
Tension falls below threshold and player has built enough rapport or context to offer a credible "out." Hostage taker stands down. Hostages released unharmed.
Escalation (lose)
Tension maxes out. Hostage taker becomes unreachable. Scene ends badly. Player gets a brief post-mortem showing which moments tipped the balance.
Stalemate (partial) under review
Time limit reached with tension mid-range. SWAT called in. Morally ambiguous outcome. Feasibility TBD — may be cut or folded into the escalation path.
Breakthrough (removed) cut
Player uncovers the root cause and addresses it directly. Rare — requires finding specific evidence chain.
De-escalation progress — 50/50 model new
Two equal pillars
Progress toward de-escalation is split evenly between physical evidence gathered and the quality of the ongoing conversation. A player who finds every clue but handles the dialogue badly will stall. A player who reads the person well and speaks carefully can make real ground without touching half the evidence. Neither path is more "correct" — the game rewards both investigative and interpersonal skill equally.
Evidence strand
Clues gathered, documents read, objects examined. Each one enriches the AI's context and unlocks new things the player plausibly knows — but never hard-gates a topic from being raised in conversation.
Conversation strand
Tone, pacing, empathy signals, avoiding triggers, catching contradictions. The AI tracks what's been said and responds to how the player handles each exchange — not just what topics they raise.
Voice input & CCTV interface new
Speaking with your actual voice
Player uses their microphone to speak. Audio is transcribed in real time (browser STT or server-side), then passed to the AI as the player's turn. Silence, hesitation, and tone are not currently modelled — the transcript is the signal. Fallback: text input remains available for accessibility or hardware limits.
Seeing the hostage taker
The hostage taker is visible via a diegetic CCTV monitor or video call screen embedded in the 3D room. Their animated state — posture, pacing, composure — reflects the underlying tension level. This makes the negotiation feel like talking to a real, present person rather than a phone voice in a void.
AI stack — tech under evaluation updated
Character AI / conversation
Primary candidate: Inworld (purpose-built character AI with persistent state, emotional modelling, and game SDK). Fallback LLMs under evaluation:
GLM (Zhipu) DeepSeek Kimi k2 Qwen
Selection criteria: believable in-character tone, short-response quality, per-session cost, and latency. Will prototype each against the same scenario and evaluate subjectively.
Voice synthesis
ElevenLabs for the hostage taker's voice output. Emotionally reactive voice parameters to be explored.
Clue discovery — organic approach updated
Clues don't sit behind locked conversational doors. Instead they operate as context enrichment — finding one makes the AI more believable when that topic arises, and may shift how the hostage taker reacts if the player demonstrates specific knowledge. The goal is that players feel smart for investigating, not that they feel blocked for not having done so.

How it works in practice: The AI's context block is updated as clues are found. If the player mentions something the character wouldn't expect them to know (because they found evidence), the AI reacts with surprise, fear, or changed strategy. If the player raises a topic without the evidence, the AI responds to their words at face value — they're not shut out, but they lack the "I know your secret" leverage.
Engagement strategy
Nudge system: If no new evidence is examined for 90 seconds, a subtle environmental cue draws attention (object glints, phone crackles with a hint). Non-diegetic hints available as opt-in.

AI as compass: The hostage taker is prompted to occasionally drop emotional breadcrumbs — references to names, places, grievances — that point toward relevant evidence without stating it.

Soft time pressure: A loose in-game clock on the wall adds dread without a hard countdown game-over.

Replayability: Because responses are generative, each conversation is unique even in the same scenario.
Art direction
Cel-shaded neo-noir — "living graphic novel"
High-contrast chiaroscuro lighting. Neon cyan, magenta, amber against near-black environments. Heavy ink outline on all geometry. 1980s urban decay — peeling wallpaper, flickering fluorescents, rain-streaked glass. The room should feel like a panel from a Frank Miller comic that the player can walk around inside.
UI language
CCTV/video call rendered as a physical in-world screen. Dossier is a literal folder on the desk. Tension expressed through environment: lighting reddens, audio crackles more, rain intensifies.
Audio direction
ElevenLabs voice for the hostage taker. Ambient: rain, distant sirens, muffled crowd. Score: minimalist jazz-influenced tension underscore reacting to conversation state.

Risks
AI reliabilityModel may break character or give nonsensical responses. Mitigation: robust system prompt, fallback canned responses for failure states, low temperature.
Voice input latencySTT + AI inference + TTS chain may feel sluggish. Mitigation: stream AI response into ElevenLabs as it generates; show CCTV character "thinking" animation during latency gap.
API costMonitor token usage per session across the full voice pipeline. Evaluate cheaper model options first. Set a session cap with graceful degradation.
No tension meter (post-v1)Environmental feedback must clearly communicate state once the meter is removed. Risk of player confusion. Mitigation: extensive playtesting before cutting the meter.
Player engagementConversation fatigue if character feels repetitive. Mitigation: rich persona, varied emotional states, environmental storytelling carrying equal weight.
Scope creepTwo-person part-time team. Strict Must Have discipline — ship one great scenario before expanding.
